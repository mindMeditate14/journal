# Quick Reference: Submitting Your Manuscript to NexusJournal

## 🚀 5 Minutes to Submission

### **Step 1: Go to Submission Form** (1 min)
```
1. Login at https://journal.mind-meditate.com
2. Click Dashboard
3. Click blue button: "Open Submission Form"
4. Pick your journal from the list
5. You're in!
```

### **Step 2: Enter Metadata** (4 min)
```
Title       → Your research question (10-300 characters)
Abstract    → What, how, found what (50-1000 characters)
Keywords    → 5-7 relevant terms separated by commas
Discipline  → Pick from dropdown (Medicine, Physics, etc.)
Methodology → Pick from dropdown (Experimental, Review, etc.)
```

**✅ Tip:** Don't overthink the title yet—you can come back and edit!

### **Step 3: Add Authors** (2 min)
```
For each author, provide:
- Full name
- Email address
- Affiliation (where they work)
- ORCID (optional but recommended)

👉 Click "+ Add Author" to add co-authors
```

**✅ Tip:** The first author is the corresponding author (gets all emails)

### **Step 4: Write Content** (20-30 min)
```
Introduction     → Why this matters & what you studied
Literature Review → What others found before you
Results         → Your data (facts only, no interpretation)
Discussion      → What your data means (compare with others)
Conclusion      → Why it's important (so what?)
```

**✅ Tip:** Don't write perfectly—fix grammar later. Just get words down!

### **Step 5: Add References & Declarations** (5 min)
```
References             → Paste your bibliography
Funding              → "No funding received" or list sources
Conflict of Interest → "None" or specify
Data Availability    → How people can access your data
```

**✅ Tip:** Copy-paste from your reference manager (Zotero, Mendeley, etc.)

### **Step 6: Review & Submit** (1 min)
```
1. See your manuscript summary
2. Check "All fields complete" ✓
3. Click green "Submit Manuscript" button
4. Confirmation email appears in inbox immediately!
🎉 DONE!
```

## 📝 What Each Section Should Say

### **Introduction** (Typical structure)
```
"In recent years, [field] has grown rapidly. Prior work by [Author] 
showed [finding]. However, [limitation] remains. This study investigates 
[research question] by [approach]. We hypothesized that [prediction]."

Example:
"Machine learning has revolutionized medical diagnosis. Studies show AI 
matches physician performance in radiology. However, few studies examine 
AI's role in traditional medicine. This study investigates whether 
machine learning can learn traditional diagnostic patterns. We hypothesized 
that neural networks trained on patient cases would achieve >90% accuracy."
```

### **Literature Review** (Key elements)
```
• What do we already know?
• Who studied this before?
• What are the conflicting findings?
• What gap does your study fill?

Don't just list papers—compare them! "Smith found X, but Jones 
disagreed, showing Y instead. Our study addresses this gap by..."
```

### **Results** (Just the facts)
```
✅ DO:
- "77% of participants reported improvement"
- "The correlation was r=0.82, p<0.001"
- "See Figure 1 for the distribution"

❌ DON'T:
- "Remarkably, participants improved significantly..."
- "This impressive result shows..."
- "We interpret this to mean..."
(Save interpretation for Discussion!)
```

### **Discussion** (The important part)
```
1. What did you find? (restate results)
2. Does it match prior research? (compare with others)
3. Why is this? (mechanisms/explanations)
4. What are the limitations? (honest assessment)
5. So what? (implications for practice/future)

Example:
"We found 94% accuracy, matching Smith et al.'s 92%. Unlike 
previous work, we included subjective features (pulse, tongue). 
Our success suggests AI can learn from non-quantitative data. 
Limitations: single institution, small sample, no prospective 
validation. These findings could help practitioners in rural areas 
with limited specialist access."
```

### **Conclusion** (The summary)
```
Keep it brief (150-300 words):
1. Main finding (one sentence)
2. Why it matters (clinical/practical implications)
3. What's next? (future research needed)

Example:
"This study demonstrated that AI can learn diagnostic patterns 
from traditional medicine records. This could augment practitioner 
training in countries where specialists are scarce. Future work 
should validate these findings prospectively with new patients."
```

## ✅ Before You Hit Submit

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Title is 10-300 characters
- [ ] Abstract is 50-1000 characters and includes what/how/found
- [ ] Abstract is CLEAR (someone outside your field understands)
- [ ] At least 1 author with email added
- [ ] All 5 main sections filled in (Intro through Conclusion)
- [ ] References listed (10+ sources typical)
- [ ] Funding declared (even if "no funding")
- [ ] Conflict of interest declared (even if "none")
- [ ] Data availability explained
- [ ] Spell-checked (use Grammarly or similar)
- [ ] Proofread by a friend if possible

## 🆘 Common Questions

**Q: How long should my introduction be?**  
A: 300-500 words. Should feel like 1 page single-spaced.

**Q: What if I don't have funding?**  
A: Write "No funding received for this research" in Funding Statement.

**Q: What's a conflict of interest?**  
A: You work for the company you're studying OR own stock OR have family member there. If none, say "None."

**Q: What if my data is proprietary?**  
A: Write "Data are proprietary and cannot be shared" or "Available by request from corresponding author."

**Q: Can I edit after I submit?**  
A: Yes! Editors often request revisions. You'll get feedback on what needs to change.

**Q: How long until I hear back?**  
A: Desk review (editor) takes ~1 week. Peer review takes 4-8 weeks total.

**Q: What if I get rejected?**  
A: Reviewers provide feedback. Revise and submit to another journal. Most papers get rejected at least once before acceptance.

## 💡 Writing Tips by Section

| Section | Tip |
|---------|-----|
| **Title** | Make it specific, not "A Study of X" but "How X Improves Y by 50%" |
| **Abstract** | Imagine explaining your paper to smart person in 2 minutes |
| **Intro** | End with a clear question: "Did X improve Y?" |
| **Lit Review** | Don't list papers, explain how they relate to your study |
| **Methods** | Write detailed enough someone else could copy you exactly |
| **Results** | Numbers first, English second ("77%, not "many people") |
| **Discussion** | Compare to other studies, admit limitations, explain why it matters |
| **Conclusion** | Make it sound important! This is what readers remember |
